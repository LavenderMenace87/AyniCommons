Section I

What We Are
Building Here

Ayni Commons is a regenerative land stewardship community being established on 40 acres of high-desert rangeland in Apache County, Arizona. It is being built on degraded soil with a compromised water table and barely any biological matter — because fixing what is broken is the point. The land is not a backdrop for the mission. The land is the mission.

The community it sustains offers something more specific: a structured, merit-based pathway through which individuals and families experiencing housing instability, homelessness, or economic displacement can find purpose, build competency, and ultimately own land. Not through charity. Through ayni — reciprocal exchange, earned trust, and the covenant we make with each other and with the earth.

"We do not inherit the land from our ancestors.
We borrow it from our children."
The Central Argument

Homelessness and housing instability are structural outputs of a system that has treated land as an investment vehicle and community as optional. The answer is not more services. It is a different relationship to land — one in which people have a role, a responsibility, and a stake. This community is designed to provide all three.

Every practice put into this ground — the water harvesting, the soil building, the food systems, the creative studios, the below-grade fungal hub brewing fertility from fish scraps and forest floor biology — is built to outlast the founder and to demonstrate that degraded high-desert land can, through deliberate stewardship, become something alive and extraordinary.

Section II

The Pathway

The pipeline from entry to land ownership is structured, merit-based, and transparent. Every tier has defined rights, defined responsibilities, and defined criteria for advancement. Progress is earned through demonstrated commitment and competency — verified collectively.

On Self-Selection

The single most important design feature of this pathway is that it begins with informed self-selection. The community's values, expectations, physical demands, and lifestyle are described plainly before anyone arrives. This is not a conventional life. People who arrive knowing what they are entering are better prepared, more committed, and more likely to thrive.

Core Farm Enterprises
The Below-Grade Fungal Hub

The walipini pit greenhouse and the fungal hub share the same below-grade zone and are physically connected. The hub operates six fertility systems: Fish Amino Acids brewed from tilapia scraps, Fermented Plant Juice from on-site comfrey, Indigenous Microorganisms captured from the forest floor, Compost Tea brewed with duck pond water, Mycorrhizal Inoculant (the only purchased input — once, then self-perpetuating forever), and Wood Chip Habitat from juniper clearing. Year 2 onward: total fertility program cost approaches zero.

The gardens are also wired for biosonification — the plants' own electrical voice plays as continuous ambient sound. The garden is not silent. It is alive.

Also: sauna, cold plunge, projector screen for movie nights, gaming when it rains. Ancient practices and modern comforts coexist without apology. The integration is the point.

Section IV-B

Living Systems

Self-sufficiency is not an ideology here. It is an engineering problem. Every system below reduces dependence on a purchased input or external utility. Taken together they constitute a community that can function — not merely survive but genuinely thrive — independent of the grid, the grocery supply chain, the fuel market, and the pharmaceutical industry.

Water Sovereignty
  • Swales + Snow Harvest

    On-contour earthworks catch monsoon rainfall. At 6,200 ft the property receives real snow — designed berms direct snowmelt into infiltration zones.

  • Fog Nets

    High-elevation mesh panels harvest moisture from fog and low cloud that never registers as rainfall. Zero maintenance, meaningful yield.

  • Greywater Cascade

    Sauna, kitchen, and shower water runs through planted biofilter beds — willow, elderberry, comfrey — before entering the orchard or pond sequence. Nothing is wasted.

  • Rain Chains

    From every roof edge into cisterns. Japanese aesthetic, fully functional, beautiful in the rain.

Energy Sovereignty
  • Rocket Mass Heaters

    The most thermally efficient wood-burning technology available. Burns a fraction of conventional stove fuel. Stores heat in thermal mass for 12–18 hours. Every earthen home gets one. Fuel: on-site juniper.

  • Biogas Digester

    Goat and pig manure plus kitchen scraps produce cooking gas and liquid fertilizer. Closes the waste loop entirely. Replaces propane.

  • Solar + Wind

    Solar primary. Small wind turbine backup for cloudy days and winter. They are complementary, not redundant.

  • Passive Solar Design

    Every structure oriented for winter solar gain. Summer sun blocked, winter sun captured. No mechanical heating needed in mild weather.

Section V

The Covenant

The title of this community is not a sentiment. Ayni is the covenant. In a market economy, what we owe each other is defined by contract, priced by the market, and enforced by courts. In this community, what we owe each other is defined by this — and enforced by the community itself.

What the Community Owes Every Member
  • Safety

    Physical safety, freedom from harassment, freedom from coercion. Non-negotiable at every tier and for every person on the property regardless of age, relationship, or status.

  • Sustenance

    Adequate food from the farm's production and adequate shelter. Not contingent on performance. Not used as leverage.

  • Voice

    Every person on this property has the right to be heard. The weight of that voice in formal decisions changes by tier. The right to speak does not.

  • A Fair Pathway

    The advancement criteria are written, published, and applied consistently. No one is held back without documented reason. No one is advanced without meeting the criteria.

  • The Promise of Ownership

    The founding steward's commitment to the ownership pathway is a founding obligation, documented in the covenant and binding in perpetuity.

What Every Member Owes the Community
  • Labor

    5–6 hours of farm work per day, 5 days per week. The foundation of the exchange. Not negotiable.

  • Care

    For the land, the animals, the infrastructure, and each other. Care is not a personality trait here — it is a practice the community teaches and expects.

  • Honesty

    About capacity, struggle, and conflict. A community that cannot speak plainly about difficulty cannot function. Problems addressed early are problems that can be solved.

Time as Community Currency

Ayni Commons operates an internal time-banking system. One hour of contribution equals one time credit, regardless of the nature of the work. An hour of goat milking equals an hour of teaching pottery equals an hour of childcare equals an hour of accounting. Time banking makes visible what the covenant already implies: every contribution has equal worth.

How It Works

Every hour of contribution beyond the daily labor requirement is logged. Credits accumulate and can be exchanged for services from other members, priority access to certain resources, or recognition in advancement considerations. The time bank is housed in the teaching platform and updated in real time.

Conflict Ritual

Separate from the conflict resolution process. A monthly community circle in which appreciation and difficulty are spoken in equal measure — not a meeting with an agenda but a structured space: what am I grateful for, what is hard for me right now, what do I need from this community. Speech that has a designated place does not need to find an undesignated one.

Protected Participants

Children and dependent family members who arrive with community members are protected participants. Their housing and food are unconditional — not contingent on their guardian's standing in the community. If a guardian leaves or is asked to leave, the community facilitates a safe transition that prioritizes the protected participant's wellbeing above all other considerations.

Section VIII

The Platform

Ayni Commons is one farm on 40 acres. The platform is how it becomes ten farms, then a hundred. Every practice, decision, failure, and discovery documented here becomes a replicable blueprint for anyone who wants to fix broken land and build something worth living in. The goal is not to scale Ayni Commons. It is to help others help the land — and themselves.

"One farm demonstrates the possibility.
The platform multiplies it."
What the App Does

The teaching platform is a community application built from the ground up around one question: how do you replicate a functioning regenerative land stewardship community without having to start from scratch every time? The answer is documentation as infrastructure. Every system Ayni Commons builds — water, soil, fertility, community governance, pipeline management, creative life — is documented in enough detail that someone with different land, different climate, and different people can adapt it to their own context.

Why Documentation Is the Work

The federal policy framework that informs this community — submitted to the Senate in March 2026 — argues that land stewardship at community scale is one of the primary mechanisms for discharging the United States' deferred obligations in housing, food systems, and economic displacement.

That argument needs proof. Not theoretical proof. Operational proof. Real numbers from real land showing real improvement over time. Ayni Commons is the proof of concept. The platform is how the proof travels.

Every soil test that shows organic matter increasing is evidence. Every family that advances through the pipeline is evidence. Every earthen home built by community hands is evidence. Every hour logged in the time bank is evidence. Every seed saved and traded is evidence.

The platform makes that evidence visible, searchable, and usable by anyone who wants to build something similar anywhere in the country. That is not a side project. That is the mission scaling.

"The mushroom network is playing right now.
Most people just cannot hear it yet."
The Vision at Scale

One functioning Ayni Commons, fully documented and openly published, is worth more to the broader project of land restoration and community sovereignty than ten undocumented operations. The platform is how a 40-acre high-desert farm in Apache County becomes a national model. The work is done on the land. The proof travels through the app.

Connection to Policy

The platform connects directly to the policy framework already submitted to Congress: the Land Stewardship Corps concept in "What We Owe Each Other" proposes a national network of documented, replicable regenerative land communities as a mechanism for addressing rural economic displacement, housing instability, and ecological degradation simultaneously. Ayni Commons is Site One. The platform is the documentation that makes Site Two, Three, and Four possible without starting from nothing.
